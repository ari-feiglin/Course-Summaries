\section{Varieties}

\subsection{Affine varieties}

In this section let $k$ be an algebraically closed field, $\fun(k^n,k)$ denote the $k$-algebra of functions $k^n\to k$,
and $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ be the $k$-algebra of polynomials over variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$.

Note that there is a natural $k$-algebra morphism
$$ \ev\colon k[x_1,\dots,x_n] \to \fun(k^n,k) $$
defined by $\ev(f)(P)=p(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ for a polynomial $f$ and $P=(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in k^n$.

For a subset $S\subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ define its {\emphcolor zero set}
$$ Z(S) = \set{P\in k^n}[f(P)=0\hbox{ for all $f\in S$}] \subseteq k^n . $$
For $f\in k^n$ we write $Z(f)$ for $Z(\set f)$ so that $Z(S)=\bigcap_{f\in S}Z(f)$.

\blemm

    \benum
        \item $Z$ is order reversing: if $S_1\subseteq S_2$ then $Z(S_1)\supseteq Z(S_2)$.
        \item $Z(S)$ is equal to $Z((S))$ ($(S)$ is the ideal generated by $S$).
        \item For a collection of subsets $\set{S_\alpha}_\alpha$:
        $$ \bigcap_\alpha Z(S_\alpha) = Z\parens{\bigcup S_\alpha} . $$
        \item For two subsets $S_1,S_2$, define $S_1S_2=\set{fg}[f\in S_1,g\in S_2]$, then
        $$ Z(S_1) \cup Z(S_2) = Z(S_1S_2) . $$
        \item $Z(1)=\emptyset$ and $Z(0)=k^n$.
    \eenum

\elemm

\bproof

    All of these points are clear.
    We prove only the fourth: $Z(S_1)\cup Z(S_2)=Z(S_1S_2)$.
    Note that the ideal generated by $S_1S_2$ is precisely the product of the ideals generated by $S_1$ and $S_2$.
    Thus $Z(S_1)$ and $Z(S_2)$ are contained in $Z(S_1S_2)$ by order-reversion.
    Conversely if $P\notin Z(S_1)\cup Z(S_2)$, then $f(P),g(P)\neq0$ for all $f\in S_1,g\in S_2$ so $(fg)(P)=f(P)g(P)\neq0$
    and thus $P\notin Z(S_1S_2)$.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    The set of zero sets forms a topology of closed sets on $k^n$.

\ecoro

\bdefn

    Define $\bA^n_k$ to be the topological space $k^n$ whose underlying topology is given by the zero sets.
    This is called the {\emphcolor Zariski topology}, and $\bA^n_k$ is the {\emphcolor $n$-dimensional affine space}.
    When $k$ is understood, we omit it and just write $\bA^n$.

    We also define $k[\bA^n]=k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.

\edefn

Note that $Z(f)$ forms a basis of closed sets: every closed set is an intersection of these basic closed sets.
Thus
$$ D(f) = \bA^n - Z(f) = \set{P\in k^n}[f(P)\neq0] . $$
forms a basis of open sets.

Note that for every $S\subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $(S)$ is generated by finitely many $f_1,\dots,f_n\in S$ since $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$
is Noetherian.
Then
$$ Z(S) = Z((S)) = Z(f_1,\dots,f_n) = \bigcap_{i=1}^n Z(f_i) . $$
Thus every open set is a finite union of basic open sets.
And so $\bA^n$ is quasicompact: if $D(f_i)$ cover $\bA^n$ then
$$ \bA^n = \bigcup_i D(f_i) = D(\set{f_i}) , $$
and as we showed (for zero sets, thus for open sets) $D(\set{f_i})$ is the finite union of $D(f_i)$s.

\bprop

    $\bA^n$ is quasicompact.

\eprop

\bexam

    The closed subsets of $\bA^1$ are precisely the finite subsets of $\bA^1$, or $\bA^1$ itself.
    Indeed, $k[x]$ is a PID so all of its ideals are of the form $Z(f)$ for a polynomial $f$.
    If $f\neq0$, then $f$ has finitely many roots so $Z(f)$ is finite.
    And for a finite subset $\set{c_1,\dots,c_n}$ of $\bA^1=k$, $f=(x-c_1)\cdots(x-c_n)$ has this set as its zero set.

    Thus the open subsets of $\bA^1$ are empty or cofinite.
    Since $k$ is infinite (it is algebraically closed), $\bA^1$ is not Hausdorff.

\eexam

\bdefn

    For a subset $Y\subseteq\bA^n$, define
    $$ \I(Y) = \set{f\in k[x_1,\dots,x_n]}[f(P)=0\hbox{ for all $P\in Y$}] . $$

\edefn

\bprop

    \benum
        \item $\I$ is order-reversing.
        \item $\I(Y)$ is a radical ideal of $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.
        \item $\I(Y_1\cup Y_2)=\I(Y_1)\cap\I(Y_2)$.
        \item For every $Y\subseteq\bA^n$, $Z(\I(Y))=\cl Y$ is the closure of $Y$ in $\bA^n$.
        \item {\emphcolor Nullstellensatz}: for every ideal $J\leq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $\I(Z(J))=\sqrt J$.
    \eenum

\eprop

\bproof

    The first three points are clear.
    For the fourth, notice $Y\subseteq Z(\I(Y))$ and $Z(\I(Y))$ is closed, so $\cl Y\subseteq Z(\I(Y))$.
    Since $\cl Y$ is closed, $\cl Y=Z(J)$ for some ideal $J$.
    So
    $$ Y \subseteq \cl Y = Z(J) \implies \I(\cl Y) = \I(Z(J)) \subseteq \I(Y) . $$
    And $J\subseteq\I(Z(J))$ clearly, so $J\subseteq\I(Y)$, and thus $\cl Y=Z(J)\supseteq Z(\I(Y))$ as desired.

    For the fifth point, since $J\leq\I(Z(J))$ which is radical, we see $\sqrt J\leq\I(Z(J))$.
    The inverse inclusion is far deeper, and we give a proof next section.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    $Z$ and $\I$ form an antitone Galois connection.

\ecoro

\bcoro

    For every proper ideal $J<k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $Z(J)$ is nonempty.

\ecoro

\bproof

    If $J\neq(1)$ then $\I(Z(J))=\sqrt J\neq(1)$, thus $Z(J)$ cannot be empty (since $\I(\emptyset)=(1)$).
    \qed

\eproof

\bnote

    Recall that a topological space is irreducible iff there are no two proper closed subsets whose union is $X$.
    In homework this was shown to be equivalent to the condition that all open subsets intersect.

\enote

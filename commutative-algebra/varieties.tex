\section{Varieties}

\subsection{Affine varieties}

In this section let $k$ be an algebraically closed field, $\fun(k^n,k)$ denote the $k$-algebra of functions $k^n\to k$,
and $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ be the $k$-algebra of polynomials over variables $x_1,\dots,x_n$.

Note that there is a natural $k$-algebra morphism
$$ \ev\colon k[x_1,\dots,x_n] \to \fun(k^n,k) $$
defined by $\ev(f)(P)=p(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ for a polynomial $f$ and $P=(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in k^n$.

For a subset $S\subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ define its {\emphcolor zero set}
$$ Z(S) = \set{P\in k^n}[f(P)=0\hbox{ for all $f\in S$}] \subseteq k^n . $$
For $f\in k^n$ we write $Z(f)$ for $Z(\set f)$ so that $Z(S)=\bigcap_{f\in S}Z(f)$.

\blemm

    \benum
        \item $Z$ is order reversing: if $S_1\subseteq S_2$ then $Z(S_1)\supseteq Z(S_2)$.
        \item $Z(S)$ is equal to $Z((S))$ ($(S)$ is the ideal generated by $S$).
        \item For a collection of subsets $\set{S_\alpha}_\alpha$:
        $$ \bigcap_\alpha Z(S_\alpha) = Z\parens{\bigcup S_\alpha} . $$
        \item For two subsets $S_1,S_2$, define $S_1S_2=\set{fg}[f\in S_1,g\in S_2]$, then
        $$ Z(S_1) \cup Z(S_2) = Z(S_1S_2) . $$
        \item $Z(1)=\emptyset$ and $Z(0)=k^n$.
    \eenum

\elemm

\bproof

    All of these points are clear.
    We prove only the fourth: $Z(S_1)\cup Z(S_2)=Z(S_1S_2)$.
    Note that the ideal generated by $S_1S_2$ is precisely the product of the ideals generated by $S_1$ and $S_2$.
    Thus $Z(S_1)$ and $Z(S_2)$ are contained in $Z(S_1S_2)$ by order-reversion.
    Conversely if $P\notin Z(S_1)\cup Z(S_2)$, then $f(P),g(P)\neq0$ for all $f\in S_1,g\in S_2$ so $(fg)(P)=f(P)g(P)\neq0$
    and thus $P\notin Z(S_1S_2)$.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    The set of zero sets forms a topology of closed sets on $k^n$.

\ecoro

\bdefn

    Define $\bA^n_k$ to be the topological space $k^n$ whose underlying topology is given by the zero sets.
    This is called the {\emphcolor Zariski topology}, and $\bA^n_k$ is the {\emphcolor $n$-dimensional affine space}.
    When $k$ is understood, we omit it and just write $\bA^n$.

    We also define $k[\bA^n]=k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.

\edefn

Note that $Z(f)$ forms a basis of closed sets: every closed set is an intersection of these basic closed sets.
Thus
$$ D(f) = \bA^n - Z(f) = \set{P\in k^n}[f(P)\neq0] . $$
forms a basis of open sets.

Note that for every $S\subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $(S)$ is generated by finitely many $f_1,\dots,f_n\in S$ since $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$
is Noetherian.
Then
$$ Z(S) = Z((S)) = Z(f_1,\dots,f_n) = \bigcap_{i=1}^n Z(f_i) . $$
Thus every open set is a finite union of basic open sets.
And so $\bA^n$ is quasicompact: if $D(f_i)$ cover $\bA^n$ then
$$ \bA^n = \bigcup_i D(f_i) = D(\set{f_i}) , $$
and as we showed (for zero sets, thus for open sets) $D(\set{f_i})$ is the finite union of $D(f_i)$s.

\bprop

    $\bA^n$ is quasicompact.

\eprop

\bexam

    The closed subsets of $\bA^1$ are precisely the finite subsets of $\bA^1$, or $\bA^1$ itself.
    Indeed, $k[x]$ is a PID so all of its ideals are of the form $Z(f)$ for a polynomial $f$.
    If $f\neq0$, then $f$ has finitely many roots so $Z(f)$ is finite.
    And for a finite subset $\set{c_1,\dots,c_n}$ of $\bA^1=k$, $f=(x-c_1)\cdots(x-c_n)$ has this set as its zero set.

    Thus the open subsets of $\bA^1$ are empty or cofinite.
    Since $k$ is infinite (it is algebraically closed), $\bA^1$ is not Hausdorff.

\eexam

\bdefn

    For a subset $Y\subseteq\bA^n$, define
    $$ \I(Y) = \set{f\in k[x_1,\dots,x_n]}[f(P)=0\hbox{ for all $P\in Y$}] . $$

\edefn

\bprop

    \benum
        \item $\I$ is order-reversing.
        \item $\I(Y)$ is a radical ideal of $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.
        \item $\I(Y_1\cup Y_2)=\I(Y_1)\cap\I(Y_2)$.
        \item For every $Y\subseteq\bA^n$, $Z(\I(Y))=\cl Y$ is the closure of $Y$ in $\bA^n$.
        \item {\emphcolor Nullstellensatz}: for every ideal $J\leq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $\I(Z(J))=\sqrt J$.
    \eenum

\eprop

\bproof

    The first three points are clear.
    For the fourth, notice $Y\subseteq Z(\I(Y))$ and $Z(\I(Y))$ is closed, so $\cl Y\subseteq Z(\I(Y))$.
    Since $\cl Y$ is closed, $\cl Y=Z(J)$ for some ideal $J$.
    So
    $$ Y \subseteq \cl Y = Z(J) \implies \I(\cl Y) = \I(Z(J)) \subseteq \I(Y) . $$
    And $J\subseteq\I(Z(J))$ clearly, so $J\subseteq\I(Y)$, and thus $\cl Y=Z(J)\supseteq Z(\I(Y))$ as desired.

    For the fifth point, since $J\leq\I(Z(J))$ which is radical, we see $\sqrt J\leq\I(Z(J))$.
    The inverse inclusion is far deeper, and we give a proof next section.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    $Z$ and $\I$ form an antitone Galois connection.

\ecoro

\bcoro

    For every proper ideal $J<k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, $Z(J)$ is nonempty.

\ecoro

\bproof

    If $J\neq(1)$ then $\I(Z(J))=\sqrt J\neq(1)$, thus $Z(J)$ cannot be empty (since $\I(\emptyset)=(1)$).
    \qed

\eproof

\bnote

    Recall that a topological space is irreducible iff there are no two proper closed subsets whose union is $X$.
    In homework this was shown to be equivalent to the condition that all open subsets intersect.

\enote

\bexam

    Every closed subset of $\bA^1$ is either $\bA^1$ or finite.
    Since $k$ is infinite, $\bA^1$'s irreducible closed subsets are its closed points.

\eexam

\bcoro

    A closed subset $Y\subseteq\bA^n$ is irreducible iff the ideal $\I(Y)\subseteq k[\bA^n]$ is either prime or all $k[\bA^n]$.

\ecoro

\bproof

    If $Y\subseteq\bA^n$ is irreducible and $fg\in\I(Y)$, then we want to show either $f\in\I(Y)$ or $g\in\I(Y)$.
    Notice that since $fg\in\I(Y)$, we have $Y=\cl Y=Z(\I(Y))\subseteq Z(fg)=Z(f)\cup Z(g)$.
    Since $Y$ is irreducible either $Y\subseteq Z(f)$ or $Y\subseteq Z(g)$, meaning $f\in\I(Y)$ or $g\in\I(Y)$ as desired.

    If $\I(Y)$ is prime or all $k[\bA^n]$, suppose $Y=Y_1\cup Y_2$ where $Y_1,Y_2\subseteq Y$ are closed.
    Then
    $$ \I(Y_1)\I(Y_2) \subseteq \I(Y_1)\cap\I(Y_2) = \I(Y) . $$
    Thus either $\I(Y_1)$ or $\I(Y_2)$ is contained in $\I(Y)$.
    Suppose $\I(Y_1)\subseteq\I(Y)$, then applying $Z$ gives $Y_1\supseteq Y$ (since they are closed), so $Y=Y_1$ as desired.
    \qed

\eproof

\bexam

    $\bA^n$ is irreducible since $\I(\bA^n)=(0)$ is prime.

    If $f\in k[\bA^n]$ is irreducible, then $Z(f)$ is irreducible in $\bA^n$.
    This is because $\I(Z(f))=\sqrt{(f)}=(f)$ is prime.

\eexam

\subsubsection*{Proof of the Nullstellensatz}

\blemm

    Let $f\in k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ be nonconstant and $A=k[x_1,\dots,x_n]/(f)$.
    Then there exist integers $k_1,\dots,k_{n-1}>0$ such that the $k$-algebra homomorphism
    $$ \phi\colon k[y_1,\dots,y_{n-1}] \to A,\qquad y_i\mapsto x_i-x_n^{k_i} $$
    is injective and finite.

\elemm

\bproof

    For an $n$-tuple $I=(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ let $x^I=x_1^{d_1}\cdots x_n^{d_n}$ and call $I$ the {\emphcolor multidegree} of $x^I$.
    We define $k_j$ for $1\leq j\leq n$ by descending recursion and setting $k_n=1$, and letting
    $$ k_j = 1 + \max_I\set{\sum_{i=j+1}^nk_id_i} , $$
    where $I=(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ ranges over all the multidegrees in $f$ with nonzero coefficient.
    We claim that $k_j$ satisfy the claim.

    Let $y_i=x_i-x_n^{k_i}$ for $1\leq i\leq n-1$, and order the multidegrees in $f$ lexicographically.
    That is, $(d_1,\dots,d_n)>(d'_1,\dots,d'_n)$ iff $d_j>d'_j$ for some index $j$ and $d_i=d'_i$ for all $i<j$.
    This is a total order, and so $f$ has a maximum multidegree $I_f=(D_1,\dots,D_n)$.

    Now define $N=\sum_{i=1}^nk_iD_i$.
    Then we claim that $f$ is a polynomial in $B[x_n]$ of degree $N>0$ in $x_n$, whose leading coefficient is in $k^\times$, where
    $B=k[y_1,\dots,y_{n-1}]\subseteq k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$.

    Let $m=x^I$ be a monomial with $I=(d_1,\dots,d_n)$.
    Substitute $x_i=y_i+x_n^{k_i}$, which shows that $m$ is a monic polynomial in $B[x_n]$ of $x_n$-degree $\sum_{i=1}^nk_id_i$.
    We claim that under this substitution, the $x_n$-degree of $x^{I_f}$ is greater than $x^I$'s for any other multidegree $I$ in $f$.
    That is,
    $$ \sum_{i=1}^n k_iD_i > \sum_{i=1}^n k_id_i $$
    for all other multidegrees $I$.
    This will then imply the claim regarding $f$, as it is of the form
    $$ f = \sum_I a_Ix^I = \sum_I a_I(y_I-x_n^{k_I})^I , $$
    and considering the combination of monomials.

    So let $I=(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ be another multidegree of $f$.
    By $I_f$'s maximality there is a $j$ such that $D_j\geq d_j+1$ and $D_i=d_i$ for $i<j$.
    Then
    $$ \sum_{i=1}^nk_iD_i \geq \sum_{i=1}^jk_iD_i \geq \sum_{i=1}^{j-1}k_id_i + k_j(d_j+1) = \sum_{i=1}^jk_id_i + k_j . $$
    By definition of $k_j$,
    $$ > \sum_{i=1}^jk_id_i + \sum_{i=j+1}^nk_id_i = \sum_{i=1}^nk_id_i $$
    as desired.

    Now we need to show that these $k_i$ actually prove the lemma.
    First we show that $\phi$ is integral; let $C=k[y_1,\dots,y_{n-1}]$ be $\phi$'s domain.
    Let $c\in k^\times$ be $f$'s leading coefficient as a polynomial in $B[x_n]$, then $c^{-1}f$ vanishes in $A$.
    Since $c^{-1}f$ is a monic polynomial in $B[x_n]$ which is zero, so $\bar x_n\in A$ is integral over $C$, as desired.
    Since $\bar x_i=\bar y_i+\bar x_n^{k_i}$, $\bar x_i\in A$ is also integral over $C$, and thus $A$ is integral over $C$ via $\phi$.
    Furthermore, $A$ is fg over $C$ and thus finite.

    Note that $x_n\mapsto x_n$, $x_i\mapsto y_i=x_i-x_n^{k_i}$ is invertible, and thus $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]=k[y_1,\dots,y_{n-1}][x_n]$,
    and $y_1,\dots,y_{n-1},x_n$ are algebraically independent.
    Now suppose $\phi(g)=0$ for some $g\in C=k[t_1,\dots,t_{n-1}]$.
    That is, $g(\bar y_1,\dots,\bar y_{n-1})=0\in A$ so $g(y_1,\dots,y_{n-1})=hf$ for some $h\in k[y_1,\dots,y_{n-1}][x_n]$.
    Since $g$ has zero in $x_n$, but $f$ has positive degree, we must have that $g=0$.
    So $\phi$ is injective.
    \qed

\eproof

\bthrm[title=Noether normalization lemma]

    Let $A\neq0$ be a fg algebra over a field $k$.
    Then there exists a $k$-subalgebra $B\subseteq A$ such that $B$ is isomorphic as a $k$-algebra to a polynomial algebra $k[y_1,\dots,y_m]$
    and $A$ is a finite $B$-algebra.

\ethrm

\bproof

    Since $A$ is fg, $A\cong k[x_1,\dots,x_n]/I$ for some ideal $I$.
    If $I=0$, then take $B=A$.
    Otherwise there is a nonconstant $f\in I$ (since otherwise $I$ is the whole polynomial ring and $A=0$).
    By the previous lemma, there is a subalgebra $B'\subseteq A'=k[x_1,\dots,x_n]/(f)$ such that $B'\cong k[x'_1,\dots,x'_{n-1}]$
    and $A'$ is finite over $B'$.

    Let $I'=I/(f)\subseteq A'$ and $J=I'\cap B'$ be ideals.
    Then $B'/J\cong k[x'_1,\dots,x'_{n-1}]/J$ is a finite subalgebra over $A'/I'=A$.
    By induction on the number of generators, the claim holds for $B'/J$, so there is a subalgebra $B\subseteq B'/J\subseteq A$ such that
    $B$ is a polynomial ring $k[y_1,\dots,y_m]$ and $B'/J$ is a finite $B$-algebra.
    Since being a finite algebra is transitive, $A$ is a finite $B$-algebra, as desired.
    \qed

\eproof

The Noether normalization lemma then implies the following result, which can be seen to be a sort of generalization of Nullstellensatz.

\bprop

    Let $L/k$ be a field extension such that $L$ is a fg $k$-algebra.
    Then $L$ is a finite extension of $k$ (i.e.\ it is a finite $k$-algebra).

\eprop

\bproof

    By Noether, $L$ contains a polynomial subalgebra over which it is finite, and thus integral.
    Let $B=k[x_1,\dots,x_n]\subseteq L$ be this algebra.
    Since $L$ is integral over $B$, $B$ must be a field (if $A\subseteq B$ is integral, $A$ is a field iff $B$ is a field), and so $n$ must be
    zero.
    Thus $k\subseteq L$ is finite, as desired.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro[title=Weak Nullstellensatz]

    Let $A\neq0$ be a fg algebra over an algebraically closed field $k$.
    Then for any maximal ideal $\m\leq A$, $A/\m\cong k$ as $k$-algebras.

\ecoro

\bproof

    Let $L=A/\m$, which is a fg $k$-algebra, so by the above proposition it must be a finite.
    Algebraically closed fields admit no proper extensions, so $A/\m\cong k$.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    Let $A\neq0$ be a fg algebra over an algebraically closed field $k$.
    Then there exists a $k$-algebra homomorphism $\psi\colon A\to k$.

\ecoro

\bproof

    Choose a maximal ideal $\m\leq A$, and consider $\psi\colon A\to A/\m\cong k$.
    \qed

\eproof

Recall that the preimage of a prime ideal is always prime, but the preimage of a maximal ideal need not be maximal.
For example under the inclusion $\bZ\inj\bQ$, consider the preimage of the maximal ideal $(0)\leq\bQ$, which is $(0)\leq\bZ$; not maximal.

\bprop

    Let $A,B$ be fg $k$-algebras, and $f\colon A\to B$ a $k$-algebra morphism.
    Then for any maximal ideal $\m\leq B$, $\m_A=f^{-1}\m\leq A$ is maximal.

\eprop

\bproof

    The map $f$ induces an injective $k$-algebra morphism $A/\m_A\inj B/\m$.
    The latter is a field and a fg $k$-algebra, hence a finite $k$-algebra and in particular integral.
    Thus $A/\m_A$ is an integral $k$-algebra as well, and since it is a domain ($\m_A$ is prime), it must then be a field.
    So $\m_A$ is maximal.
    \qed

\eproof

\bdefn

    A ring $A$ is {\emphcolor Jacobson} when $\jac(A)=\nil(A)$.

\edefn

In general we have $\nil(A)\subseteq\jac(A)$, since $\nil(A)$ is the intersection of all prime and $\jac(A)$ all maximal ideals.

\bprop

    Every fg algebra over a field $k$ is Jacobson.

\eprop

\bproof

    Let $A$ be a fg algebra over $k$, and $f\notin\nil(A)$.
    We then claim $A$ has a maximal ideal not containing $f$.
    Since $f\notin\nil(A)$, $A_f\neq0$ as the localizing set does not contain zero, and thus contains a maximal ideal $\m$.
    Recall that $A_f\cong A[x]/(fx-1)$, so $A_f$ is a fg $k$-algebra.
    Let $\iota\colon A\to A_f$ be the canonical map, then $\iota^{-1}\m$ is maximal in $A$.
    Since $\iota f\in A_f^\times$, $f$ is not in $\iota^{-1}\m$ as desired.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    If $A$ is a fg algebra over a field $k$, then for every ideal $I\leq A$,
    $$ \sqrt I = \jac_A(I) , $$
    where $\jac_A(I)$ is the Jacobson radical of $I$, the intersection of all maximal ideals containing $I$.
    In particular for a prime $\p\leq A$,
    $$ \p = \bigcap_{\p\leq\m<A}\m . $$

\ecoro

\bproof

    This amounts to saying that $A/I$ is a Jacobson ring, since pulling back $\nil(A/I)$ and $\jac(A/I)$ give $\sqrt I,\jac_A(I)$ respectively.
    Since $A$ is a fg algebra over $k$, so is $A/I$ and the result follows the above proposition.
    \qed

\eproof

\bnote

    Some authors define a Jacobson ring to be one where $\sqrt I=\jac_A(I)$ for all ideals, or $\p=\jac_A(\p)$ for all prime ideals (these are equivalent).
    Note that this definition is more useful, but not how it was defined in this course.
    The above corollary shows that a fg algebra over a field is Jacobson in this regard as well.

\enote

Note that trhere is a natural bijection between $\bA^n$ and $k$-algebra morphisms $\phi\colon k[\bA^n]\to k$.
\benum
    \item For every $P=(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in\bA^n$ define $\phi$ by $\phi(x_i)=a_i$.
    That is, $\phi$ is the evaluation map $\ev_P$ given by $\ev_P(f)=f(P)$.
    In particular,
    $$ \ker\ev_P = \I(P) . $$
    \item Conversely for $\phi\colon k[\bA^n]\to k$, let $P=(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ by $a_i=\phi(x_i)$.
    Then $\phi=\ev_P$, so these are inverses.
\eenum

Let $J\leq k[\bA^n]$ be an ideal, then for every $P\in\bA^n$, we have $P\in Z(J)$ iff $J\leq\I(P)=\ker\ev_P$ iff
$\ev_P$ quotients over $J$: it induces $\bar\ev_P\colon k[\bA^n]/J\to k$ where $\bar\ev_P(f+J)=f(P)$.

\bprop

    For $P=(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in\bA^n$ we have
    $$ \ker\ev_P = (x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n) , $$
    which is a maximal ideal.

\eprop

\bproof

    Since $\ev_P(x_i-a_i)=0$ we have inclusion $\supseteq$.
    Since $k[\bA^n]/\ker\ev_P\cong\im\ev_P\cong k$, $\ker\ev_P$ is maximal, it is sufficient to show $k[\bA^n]/(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)\cong k$.
    Equivalently, the natural map $\iota\colon k\to k[\bA^n]/(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$ is surjective.

    The projection $k[\bA^n]\to k[\bA^n]/(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$ maps $x_i$ to $\bar x_i=\iota(a_i)$.
    Thus $\iota$ must be surjective as the projection is.
    \qed

\eproof

The following is another version of the Nullstellensatz:

\bprop

    Let $k$ be algebraically closed.
    Then the maximal ideals of $k[\bA^n]$ are precisely the ideals $\ker\ev_P$ for $P\in\bA^n$.

\eprop

\bproof

    We saw that $\ker\ev_P$ are maximal, so let $\m$ be maximal.
    By the weak Nullstellensatz, $k[\bA^n]/k\cong k$, which gives a projection $f\colon k[\bA^n]\to k[\bA^n]/\m\cong k$ with
    $\ker f=\m$.
    But $k$-algebra morphisms $k[\bA^n]\to k$ are all of the form $\ev_P$ for some $P$, so $f=\ev_P$ and thus $\m=\ker\ev_P$.
    \qed

\eproof

Now we can finally prove Nullsellensatz itself.

\bproof[Nullstellensatz]

    We already showed $\sqrt J\leq\I(Z(J))$, we now show the other direction.
    Let $f\in\I(Z(J))$, and consider $A=k[\bA^n]/J$.
    By the above proposition, the maximal ideals of $A$ are the ideals of the form $\ker\ev_P$ containing $J$, so $\ker\ev_P$ with $P\in Z(J)$.
    Because $f\in\I(Z(J))$, $f$ vanishes at every point $P\in Z(J)$, so $f\in\ker\ev_P$ for every $P\in Z(J)$.
    Thus $f$ is in every maximal ideal of $A$, so $\bar f\in\jac(A)$.
    $A$ is a fg $k$-algebra it is Jacobson, so $\bar f\in\jac(A)=\nil(A)=\sqrt J/J$, thus $f\in\sqrt J$ as desired.
    \qed

\eproof

Let us summarize the following formulations of Nullstellensatz:

\bthrm[title=Equivalent formulations of Nullstellensatz]

    The following are all equivalent formulations of Nullstellensatz, and all are true.
    \benum
        \item Nullstellensatz: $\I(Z(J))=\sqrt J$,
        \item If $J\neq(1)$ then $Z(J)\neq\emptyset$,
        \item Weak Nullstellensatz: if $A$ is fg, $A/\m\cong k$,
        \item If $A\neq0$ is fg, then there is a $\phi\colon A\to k$,
        \item Every maximal ideal of $k[\bA^n]$ of the form $(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$.
    \eenum

\ethrm

\bdefn

    For a topological space $X$, let $\abs X$ denote the subspace of closed points.

\edefn

Recall that for an affine scheme $\spec(A)$, a closed point is just a maximal ideal, so $\abs X\cong\specm(A)$.

\bcoro

    Let $k$ be algebraically closed, and $I\leq k[\bA^n]$ an ideal.
    Then there is a bijection between the closed points of $\spec(k[\bA^n]/I)$ and $Z(I)$:
    $$ \abs{\spec(k[\bA^n]/I)} \cong Z(I) . $$

\ecoro

\bproof

    The closed points of the spectrum of $k[\bA^n]/I$ are in bijection with the maximal ideals of $k[\bA^n]$ containing $I$.
    These are of the form $(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$, and if it contains $I$ then for $f\in I$ we must have that $f(a_1,\dots,a_n)=0$.
    So mapping $(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$ to $(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in Z(I)$ is well-defined and clearly injective.
    Furthermore it is clearly surjective.
    \qed

\eproof

\bcoro

    Let $A,B$ be fg $k$-algebras for some field $k$, and let $\phi\colon A\to B$ be a $k$-algebra morphism.
    Then $\phi^*\colon\spec(B)\to\spec(A)$ restricts to a morphism of closed points $\abs{\phi^*}\colon\abs{\spec(B)}\to\abs{\spec(A)}$.

\ecoro

\bproof

    This is just a restatement of the preimage of a maximal ideal being maximal.
    \qed
